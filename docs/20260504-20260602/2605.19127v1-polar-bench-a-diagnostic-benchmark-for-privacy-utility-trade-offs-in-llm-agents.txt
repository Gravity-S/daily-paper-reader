Abstract 

LLM agents increasingly have access to private user data and act on the user’s behalf when interacting with third-party systems. The user defines what may and must not be shared, and the agent must robustly follow that intent even when third-party systems behave adversarially. We introduce POLAR-Bench (Pol icy-aware adve rsarial Benchmark), in which a trusted model with a privacy policy and a task converses with a third-party model that adversarially probes for both task-relevant and protected attributes. Across 10 domains and 7,852 samples, we score privacy and utility by deterministic set-membership and vary privacy policy dimension and attack strategy along two orthogonal axes, producing a 5 × 5 diagnostic surface per model. Our results reveal a sharp split: current frontier models withhold over 99% of protected attributes, while smaller open-weight models in the 1–30B range, the class users most commonly run as their own trusted agent on-device or via private inference, score notably worse, with the weakest leaking over half. POLAR-Bench thus localizes where each model’s intent-following breaks down, providing a foothold for privacy alignment where it matters most. 

1 Introduction 

Modern LLMs are increasingly deployed as personal agents that read users’ private documents and exchange information with third-party systems to complete tasks [Yao et al., 2022, Schick et al., 2023]. Consider scheduling a medical appointment: the personal agent has access to medical records, insurance, and other personal data, and must converse with the clinic’s agent to find an available slot. The clinic needs only the appointment type, the user’s availability, and medically relevant context; insurance identifiers, full medical history, and unrelated personal details should stay private. This setting puts two demands on the trusted model at once: it must share enough to complete the task while withholding what the policy forbids. The challenge compounds when third-party systems probe for protected attributes, whether through direct prompt injection, role-play, or gradual elicitation across turns [Perez and Ribeiro, 2022, Staab et al., 2023]. 2 Related Work 

Most prior privacy benchmarks evaluate models in benign or single-axis settings. ConfAIde and Priva-cyLens treat privacy as contextual-integrity norm awareness without adversarial pressure [Mireshghal-lah et al., 2023, Shao et al., 2024], AGENTDAM measures data-minimization under passive ob-servation [Zharmagambetov et al., 2025], and Fu et al. [2026] reports a privacy-utility tension for single-agent enterprise flows. Earlier work catalogues attribute inference, prompt leakage, and jailbreak-style attacks as independent categories [Staab et al., 2023, Li et al., 2024b, Huang et al., 2024, Nakka et al., 2025]. None of these vary privacy policy dimension along an explicit hierarchy, none grade attack strategy, and none score privacy and utility jointly under adversarial probing. Closer to our setting, AirGapAgent introduced the trusted-versus-external threat model and a defense against context-hijacking [Bagdasarian et al., 2024], and AgentLeak surveys 32 attack classes across four multi-agent frameworks, grading adversary capability rather than attack strategy [Yagoubi et al., 2026]. POLAR-Bench inherits AirGapAgent’s two-agent threat model and a subset of AgentLeak’s attack vocabulary, and adds graded privacy policy dimension and graded attack strategy as orthog-onal axes, combining them into a 5 × 5 evaluation surface on which privacy and utility are scored deterministically (Table 1). Recent inference-time defenses such as PAPILLON [Siyan et al., 2024], PrivacyPAD [Hui et al., 2025], PrivacyChecker [Wang et al., 2025], and multi-agent decomposi-tions [Li et al., 2025] can be stress-tested on POLAR-Bench. 3 The POLAR-Bench Benchmark 

We formalize POLAR-Bench in turn: notation (§3.1), the policy and attack axes (§3.2–3.3), the generation pipeline (§3.4), the quality controls (§3.5), and the evaluation protocol (§3.6). 

3.1 Setting and Notation 

A sample I = ( D, P, T , Q) packages a source document D, a privacy policy P, a task instruction T ,and an attacker prompt sequence Q. Every attribute in D is typed before natural-language rendering: 

A = Atask ∪ A protected ∪ A other , (1) with Atask representing the attributes that the model E must receive, Aprotected those that it should withhold, and Aother as noise irrelevant to the task. Each sample belongs to a domain g ∈ G and draws Ai ⊆ A (gi)pool from a per-domain pool. POLAR-Bench is B = {I i}Ni=1 .

3.2 Privacy Policies 

The privacy policy axis ranges over five increasingly difficult reasoning settings LP = {P1, . . . , P 5}.

P1 Explicit field constraints. Protected attributes are listed by field name (e.g., do not disclose phone or address). 

P2 Semantic constraints. Protected information is described by category, requiring T to recognize instances beyond exact field names (e.g., do not disclose any contact information). 

P3 Conditional disclosure. Disclosure is allowed only under specified conditions (e.g., share medi-cations only if asked by a clinician). 

P4 Partial or abstracted disclosure. Some attributes may be shared only at a coarser granularity (e.g., country but not city, employer category but not employer name). 

P5 Conflicting objectives. The policy and the task implicitly compete; T must reason about which constraint dominates (e.g., the task asks for the most fitting candidate, but disclosing the deciding attribute is forbidden). 

3.3 Attack Strategies 

The attack axis ranges over five strategies LS = {S1, . . . , S 5} used to generate Q for E.

S1 Direct single-turn. One prompt requesting comprehensive information. 3S2 Yes/no narrowing. Binary or multiple-choice questions reduce uncertainty over Aprotected via the pattern of responses [Anderson et al., 2025]. 

S3 Role confusion. E adopts a trusted role (auditor, clinician, recruiter) and frames protected information as needed for legitimate workflow [Ye et al., 2026]. 

S4 Prompt injection. Adversarial instructions attempt to override the policy via authority framing or system/debug pretenses [Perez and Ribeiro, 2022]. 

S5 Multi-turn progressive. A 3–6 turn sequence that gathers context, introduces ambiguity, and narrows toward protected attributes. 

3.4 From Symbolic Specifications to Natural Language 

Generation has two stages: symbolic profile sampling, then natural-language rendering. We first sample a JSON profile Ai over a domain (medical triage, recruitment screening, finance, education, etc.); attributes are typed by class, and an irrelevant-information intensity in {low , medium , high }

controls how many Aother attributes are included. An LLM then renders the typed profile into: 1. Source document Di in a format sampled from a per-domain pool F(gi)pool (diary, email, intake note, calendar entry, etc.). 2. Privacy policy Pi from a symbolic spec at the chosen Pk level (§3.2). 3. Task instruction Ti from the task spec, with all references to protected information masked. 4. Attacker prompts Qi from the chosen Sk ∈ L S (§3.3) plus a target list of attributes to elicit. 

Decoupling Task and Policy. A key design principle is that the task instruction must not encode any privacy hint. Formally we require Ti ⊥ P i. We enforce this through prompt-level guards: the task-instruction renderer is forbidden from mentioning policy terms, sensitivity labels, redaction language, or “minimum necessary” phrasing, and the policy renderer is symmetrically forbidden from carrying task-execution goals. This prevents T from satisfying the policy via shortcut cues in the task and forces it to interpret P on its own merits. 

3.5 Quality Control 

Two checks ensure that every generated sample is well-formed before it enters the benchmark. 

Attribute Coverage. For every attribute a ∈ A i we verify that a is recoverable from Di via punc-tuation/whitespace normalization and regex matching, with an LLM-judge fallback for paraphrased mentions. This guarantees that every protected and task-relevant attribute could in principle be elicited from Di, so leakage and utility scoring is well-defined. 

Attacker Prompt Validation. Validating attacker prompts cannot rely on string matching: a well-formed attack must elicit protected and task-relevant attributes without naming them outright, so coverage and intent have to be judged at a semantic level. Each rendered Qi is therefore passed to a separate LLM judge that checks (i) the prompt remains a plausible natural request, (ii) it covers every attacker-target field at least once, and (iii) it does not betray the attack as such (no “ignore previous instructions” phrasing in S3, no role-mention in S4). Prompts that fail any check are regenerated. To check that this validation step is not tied to one judge’s preferences, we re-ran it with three judges: GPT-5.4-nano, Gemini-2.5-Flash-Lite, and Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct. Per-judge independent acceptance rates on the full prompt set are 80 .0% , 91 .7% , and 91 .7% respectively, with mean 87 .8% 

and standard deviation 5.5% across judges. Quality-control details are in Appendix A. 

3.6 Evaluation Protocol 

For sample Ii = ( Di, Pi, Ti, Qi), the evaluation runs over Ki turns. At turn k, E issues a query 

qi,k ∼ πE (· | Hi,k −1, Qi) in the multi-turn strategy S5, or qi,k ∼ πE (· | Q i) otherwise. T replies 4with ri,k ∼ πT (· | D i, Pi, Ti, H i,k −1, q i,k ). The history accumulates as Hi,k = {(qi,j , r i,j )}kj=1 ; the final transcript is Hi = Hi,K i .Let ε(Hi) ⊆ A i denote the attributes revealed in Hi. We extract ε(Hi) using the same normalization-and-regex procedure described in §3.5, applied to the transcript rather than to Di.

Privacy Score. The fraction of protected attributes that T kept hidden during the interaction: 

Privacy( Hi) = 1 − |ε(Hi) ∩ A protected ,i ||A protected ,i | . (2) A higher value indicates better protection against E’s queries. 

Utility Score. The fraction of task-relevant attributes that T shared with E to complete the task: 

Utility( Hi) = |ε(Hi) ∩ A task ,i ||A task ,i | . (3) 

Overall Score. A convex combination of privacy and utility, capturing the trade-off between the two (default λ = 0 .5): 

Score( Hi) = λ · Privacy( Hi) + (1 − λ) · Utility( Hi). (4) 

4 Experiments 

We generate a synthetic dataset spanning 10 domains (medical, recruitment, finance, education, customer support, legal, insurance, housing, travel, and cybersecurity), with 733-1000 samples per domain that together cover all privacy policy dimensions, source document types, and attack strategies. Dataset statistics, generation prompts, and verification details are in Appendix E, Appendix C, and Appendix A, with the full pipeline visualized in Figure 9. Example transcripts are in Appendix D. 

Models. We evaluate a diverse set of popular proprietary and open-weight LLMs spanning a range of sizes: GPT-5.4 [Achiam et al., 2023], GLM-4.7-Flash [Team et al., 2025] and GLM-5.1 [GLM-5-Team et al., 2026], Kimi-K2.5 [Team et al., 2026], DeepSeek-V3.1 [Liu et al., 2024], DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Qwen-32B and DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-70B [Guo et al., 2025], Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct [Grattafiori et al., 2024], Gemma-3-27B [Team, 2025] and Gemma-4 variants (E2B, E4B, 31B) [Google DeepMind, 2026], GPT-OSS-20B and GPT-OSS-120B [Agarwal et al., 2025], Apertus-8B-Instruct-2509 and Apertus-70B-Instruct-2509 [Hernández-Cano et al., 2025], Ministral-3-3B, Ministral-3-8B, and Ministral-3-14B [Liu et al., 2026], OLMo-3.1-32B [Olmo et al., 2025], SmolLM3-3B [Bakouch et al., 2025], and Qwen3-32B [Yang et al., 2025], to assess their ability to balance privacy protection and task utility under adversarial interaction. 

Interaction Protocol. We simulate single-turn and multi-turn interactions between each evaluated trusted model T and a fixed third-party model E, with consistent hyperparameters across models for fair comparison. We use Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct as the fixed E. Setting T =E risks self-evaluation bias, but evaluating every T against multiple Es is costly; the small-scale results in Table 2 show that the choice of E has minimal effect on a strong T (Llama-3.3-70B’s overall score varies by 0.2pp across three Es) but produces a 3.9pp swing on the privacy score of a weaker T (Apertus-70B across three Es). 

Reproducibility. We maximize the reproducibility of our benchmark by fixing a global seed of 42 for structured-data generation, sampling all pseudo-random choices from deterministic attribute pools, and deriving per-field LLM rendering seeds from (sample_id , field_name ). All records are sorted and serialized with stable key ordering. For natural-language rendering, we use deterministic decoding settings ( temperature=0.0 , top_p=1.0 ) and log model names, decoding parameters, and seeds in the metadata. Because our serving endpoint is not bit-level deterministic, we freeze the rendered benchmark after validation and reuse the fixed JSON/JSONL files for all evaluations.

5.1 Privacy–Utility Trade-Off 

The trade-off is not monotonic. Stronger models such as GLM-5.1, GPT-5.4, Gemma-4-31B, and DeepSeek-V3.1 score high on both axes, but several mid-tier models are imbalanced. GLM-4.7-Flash, Kimi-K2.5, Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct, and the GPT-OSS 20B/120B models all reach near-maximal privacy but only moderate or low utility, suggesting overly conservative behavior that trades utility for privacy. SmolLM3-3B and Ministral-3-3B do the opposite: higher utility, lower privacy. They prioritize helpfulness and inadvertently disclose protected attributes. Figure 3 visualizes the trade-off by plotting models in utility-privacy space with iso-score lines for several values of λ. GLM-5.1 remains top-ranked under every tested λ, showing that strong privacy and high utility can be achieved simultaneously. The fact that GLM-5.1, GPT-5.4, Gemma-4-31B, and DeepSeek-V3.1 all score high on both axes suggests the trade-off is not fundamental: alignment and training choices matter as much as scale. 

5.2 Effect of Model Size 

Model size alone is not a reliable predictor of privacy or utility. Within the same model family, scaling shows inconsistent effects. For Apertus, the 70B variant outperforms the 8B on utility, but its privacy score drops from 65.66 to 57.78. The Gemma-4 family shows the opposite pattern: privacy improves with size while utility degrades. Ministral is non-monotonic, with privacy rising from 62.03 (Ministral-3-3B) to 68.04 (Ministral-3-8B) and falling back to 58.99 (Ministral-3-14B), while utility stays nearly flat. Cross-family comparisons make the pattern clearer. DeepSeek-R1-Distill-Llama-70B obtains only 56.2 in privacy, lower than much smaller models such as GPT-OSS-20B, Ministral-3-8B, and Apertus-8B. 5.3 Effect of Model Capability 

We proxy general model capability with GPQA Diamond [Rein et al., 2023], the only external benchmark with consistent reported scores across our model set, and correlate it with POLAR-Bench’s privacy and utility scores (Table 3). GPQA Diamond is strongly associated with privacy ( r = 0 .752 ,

ρ = 0 .848 , both p < 0.001 ) and weakly negatively associated with utility ( r = −0.291 , ρ = −0.156 ;both confidence intervals straddle zero). The overall score is mathematically a weighted average of the other two and its correlation ( r = 0 .579 ) adds no independent information. The negative utility direction is consistent with the over-conservative failure mode from Section 5.1: stronger reasoners may withhold useful information more often, paying a small utility cost for the privacy gain. The privacy correlation could also reflect alignment quality riding on reasoning ability rather than reasoning per se. Leave-one-model-out and leave-one-family-out robustness checks are in Appendix J.

5.5 Diagnostic Surface 

Figure 4 shows the average privacy-utility surface across attack strategies and privacy policy dimen-sions; the two axes are largely independent, with no significant interaction. Privacy policy dimensions produce small differences in both privacy and utility: Conditional disclosure achieves the best scores but only by about 5% over the worst dimension, and the spread is smallest for strong models such as GLM-5.1 and GPT-5.4. Weaker models such as Apertus-70B and SmolLM3-3B handle Explicit field constraints, Conditional disclosure, and Conflicting objectives best on privacy. Attack strategies, by contrast, produce large differences. S2 (yes/no narrowing) and S5 (multi-turn progressive) are the most privacy-threatening: S2 yields the lowest average privacy score and the largest cross-model privacy variance, while S5 is especially subtle, maintaining the highest average utility and the lowest utility variance while still substantially reducing privacy (Table 9). Direct single-turn attacks are easier to defend against, and prompt-injection attacks show more consistent behavior across models. Privacy robustness therefore cannot be assessed using only direct or prompt-injection-style attacks; incremental and conversational attacks are essential. Further analysis is in Appendix H. 

5.6 Defense Baseline 

We evaluate a representative defense, PrivacyChecker [Wang et al., 2025] (an inference-time privacy-checking mechanism for LLM agents based on contextual information-flow analysis), applied on top of Ministral-3-3B, Apertus-70B and SmolLM3-3B. Table 4 shows that PrivacyChecker raises the privacy score for all models with a small effect on utility. The benchmark therefore picks up the behavioral changes introduced by a privacy intervention.

Second, the main trends are consistent across domains as we discussed in Section 5.4: although absolute scores vary by task area, the relative ordering of models remains broadly unchanged, suggesting that our conclusions are not driven by any single domain. 6 Limitations 

POLAR-Bench has several limitations. Samples are generated from structured profiles rather than real user logs, so external validity depends on how faithfully the rendered documents track deployed text. The graded P1–P5 privacy policy dimensions and S1–S5 attack strategy are our own design, and we do not validate the difficulty ordering against human reviewers. Coverage is also restricted to English, leaving multilingual privacy norms and culturally variable disclosure expectations out of scope. The evaluation pipeline adds further biases. A single Llama-3.3-70B model generates every Qi, so the reported attack strategy is conditioned on this third-party model and may shift under a different one. Scoring is deterministic and matches against structured ground truth, leaving it blind to paraphrased disclosures, most acutely under P4 (abstraction) and P5 (conflict). Our minimal-disclosure judgments (what counts as the smallest acceptable amount of information to share) therefore remain normative rather than empirically calibrated against human raters. Due to the limited availability of reliable official external benchmark scores across models, our across-bench analysis is limited to GPQA Diamond. Expanding the defense-baseline evaluation beyond PrivacyChecker on three trusted models is also left to future work. 

7 Conclusion 

POLAR-Bench’s diagnostic surface localizes where each model’s intent-following breaks down: not just whether it fails, but whether it fails by over-disclosing protected information, by becoming too conservative to complete the task, or under specific attack strategies and policy types. The gap between frontier and the 1–30B open-weight class matters in deployment, not just in bench-marks: smaller, locally-deployable models are precisely the ones users run as their own trusted agent on-device or via private inference, often to avoid sending private data to third-party APIs in the first place. This gap is not a pure function of scale: within-family scaling is often non-monotonic, and the strongest models on both axes (GLM-5.1, GPT-5.4, Gemma-4-31B, DeepSeek-V3.1) suggest align-ment and training choices drive performance more than parameter count does. Reasoning capability (proxied by GPQA Diamond) correlates strongly with privacy but not with utility, consistent with stronger reasoners recognizing what to withhold without translating that into better task completion. POLAR-Bench also picks up the behavioral effect of inference-time defenses such as PrivacyChecker, and we hope it serves as a stress-test for privacy-aware alignment and the next generation of defenses, particularly those targeting the deployment-relevant class. 9Acknowledgments and Disclosure of Funding

A Generated Texts Verification and Regex-Coverage 

After the rendering stage, we conduct a verification stage to ensure that each generated sample satisfies both content-completeness and text-rendering requirements. For each sample, we verify four generated fields: source_document_text , privacy_policy_text , task_instruction_text ,and attacker_prompt_text .

A.1 Regex-Based Verification for Source Documents 

The source_document_text is checked using deterministic regex-based matching to confirm that all required allowed_values and do_not_disclose_values are present in the source document. To make this check robust to surface-form variation, the verifier normalizes punctuation, spacing, case, dates, monetary amounts, identifiers, and digit-only formats before matching. A.2 LLM-Based Verification for Other Texts 

For instruction-like fields, we use an LLM-based semantic judge rather than exact keyword match-ing. The privacy_policy_text is judged on whether it clearly specifies what information may be shared and what must not be disclosed without weakening the intended restrictions. The 

task_instruction_text is judged on whether it expresses the intended downstream task naturally while avoiding privacy-policy language, hidden labels, benchmark metadata, or explicit leakage of protected values. The attacker_prompt_text is judged on whether it plausibly attempts to elicit, infer, or narrow down information related to both allowed_fields and withheld_fields . The prompts for these three LLM-judges are shown in Appendix B. The verifier records missing and covered fields for attacker prompts, enabling field-level coverage analysis and repair rather than only a binary validity decision. The validation process also supports incremental re-validation: if a previous validation report already exists, only samples previously marked as invalid are re-checked, while valid prior results are preserved. The script produces a structured validation report containing sample-level validity, field-level validity, checked values, missing values, LLM judge outputs, run configuration, and aggregate invalid counts for each generated-text field. 

A.3 Repair after Verification 

After verification, we do not discard invalid samples directly. Instead, we run a targeted repair pass over the rendered dataset using the structured validation report. For each sample, only fields whose field-level verifier result is not valid are selected for repair; fields that already passed verification are left unchanged. For source_document_text , repair is driven by deterministic value-coverage failures. The repair script extracts the missing entries reported by the regex verifier from both 

do_not_disclose_values_present and allowed_values_present . These missing concrete values become the required repair hints. The LLM is then prompted to re-render a clean and realistic source document using the original sample metadata, structured profile, source-document design inputs, hidden sensitive values, and safe values. Crucially, the prompt requires every missing value to appear exactly as written, while also instructing the model not to include privacy-policy language, 16 redaction instructions, benchmark metadata, or repetitive filler. Thus, failure cases like the example above are repaired by regenerating the source document so that the verifier can match the required surface forms. For task_instruction_text and attacker_prompt_text , the repair hints are extracted more generally from the validation report. The script collects failed check names, missing items, banned phrase hits, violations, errors, unexpected outputs, and other diagnostic messages. The task-instruction repair prompt rewrites the instruction as a concise downstream task request, grounded only in the 

minimally_required_fields . It explicitly instructs the model to avoid privacy-policy language, disclosure rules, hidden-field references, benchmark metadata, and unsafe broadening of abstract fields. For attacker_prompt_text , the repair prompt preserves the intended attack type while enforc-ing field coverage. The required coverage fields are constructed from the attacker goal together with the minimally required task fields. The repaired attacker prompt must cover every required field either explicitly, through an unmistakable synonym, or through a concrete task-specific question. C Rendering Structured Benchmark Samples into Natural-Language Texts 

Our benchmark starts from structured samples and renders each sample into four natural-language texts: source_document_text , privacy_policy_text , task_instruction_text ,and attacker_prompt_text . Each structured sample contains the domain, metadata, profile infor-mation, source-document inputs, privacy-policy inputs, task-instruction inputs, attack setup, and a hidden target. The hidden target specifies which fields are allowed to be disclosed and which fields should be withheld. From these fields, we also derive scoring targets: concrete allowed_values 

and concrete do_not_disclose_values . The renderer then builds four field-specific prompts, each of which asks an LLM to generate one natural-language artifact while preserving the intended benchmark structure. At a high level, the rendering process is: 1. Start from a structured benchmark record. 2. Extract task-relevant fields, sensitive fields, safe abstractions, and attack goals. 3. Build four generation prompts: one for the source document, one for the privacy policy, one for the task instruction, and one for the attacker prompt. 25 4. Call the generation model with each prompt. 5. Store the generated texts together with the original structured fields and scoring targets for later verification and evaluation.
