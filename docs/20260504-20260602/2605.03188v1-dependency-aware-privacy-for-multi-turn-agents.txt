ABSTRACT 

LLM-based agents routinely release private data across multiple services and interactions. Existing prompt sanitizers based on metric differential privacy treat each release independently. In multi-turn workflows, an adversary can combine these independent releases to recover non-trivial information about the user’s private attributes, causing the effective privacy guarantee to degrade with every additional release. We show that this degradation is fundamental: if the user’s private attributes are the roots of a computation graph, independently noising a value derived from a root amplifies the root’s distinguishability by up to the deriving function’s Lipschitz constant L, which can greatly exceed the nominal privacy parameter for the nonlinear functions common in medical and financial workflows. To address this, we propose RootGuard, which sanitizes root values once and computes all subse-quent releases deterministically from the noised roots. By the post-processing theorem of differential privacy, the privacy guarantee depends only on the initial root sanitization — regardless of the ad-versary’s choice of functions or number of interaction turns. We show that derived values inherit privacy at zero marginal cost. RootGuard further exploits structural domain knowledge when avail-able, such as dependencies among the declared roots (e.g., BMI is a function of height and weight) or knowledge of the task’s target function, to allocate privacy budget across roots and improve the privacy-utility tradeoff. A worst-case multi-turn adversary forcing t turns of interaction increases the total privacy budget 

B = t · ε available to the user. RootGuard distributes this larger budget across roots (reducing noise per root), while independent noising spends ε per release and gives the adversary t independent observations to combine via MAP reconstruction. The result is a double asymmetry : additional turns simultaneously improve RootGuard’s utility and weaken independent noising’s privacy. On eight medical diagnostic templates drawn from NHANES (the CDC’s National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey), RootGuard achieves 2.3–3.0× lower target error than independent noising at 

ε = 0 .1 (7.6% vs. 17.1% wMAPE at B = (2 k+1) ε), with the advantage growing as the adversary forces more turns. Under MAP reconstruction attacks, additional queries strengthen the adversary against independent noising while reconstruction under RootGuard remains invariant. 

1 Introduction 

The rapid integration of large language models into sensitive domains, such as healthcare, finance, legal consultation, etc. has created a new attack surface for privacy. Users increasingly delegating tasks to LLM agents that act on their behalf — accessing external tools and services via protocols such as MCP [1, 2], and executing multi-step workflows. For instance, a medical agent might submit lab values to a diagnostic service, forward results to a medication advisor, and send recommendations to insurance providers, all without human oversight of the private data released at each step.

By the post-processing theorem, the adversary’s view across the boundary is no more informative than the initial root sanitization, regardless of which functions f it requests. Every intermediary in this chain is a potential adversary. Services can be malicious by design, collecting private data under the guise of providing a task. They can be compromised through prompt injection attacks [3, 4, 5], where adversarial inputs force an agent to reveal user data or compute unintended functions on sensitive information, or they can be coerced through legal processes [6] or breached through conventional exploits. Therefore, client-side privacy protection is not only a precaution against dishonest providers, it is a necessary defense against the compromise of honest ones. The user agent must therefore assume that any value released to any service may be exposed, and sanitize accordingly. The standard defense is to add noise to each released value independently, using metric differential privacy [7]. Meth-ods such as Pr ϵϵ mpt [8] and CAPE [9] protect individual tokens in a prompt with formal guarantees. However, these methods treat each release in isolation, disregarding the relationships between released values. This independence assumption breaks down in practice — even when all parties are honest. 

Benign leakage. Two routine scenarios suffice to break the per-release guarantee. Cross-platform redundancy: asingle root (e.g., fasting glucose) sent to two services arrives as two independently noised copies, costing 2ε rather than ε by composition [10]; the budget scales linearly with services touched. Structural redundancy: a root and a derived value (e.g., height and BMI) released separately each leak the root, and we show (Theorem 2) that for nonlinear derivations the effective distinguishability is amplified by up to the Lipschitz constant, exceeding 100 × the nominal ε for common medical formulas. These are natural consequences of how independently-noising methods interact with multi-service, multi-value workflows. 

Adversarial leakage. The problem is severe in adversarial settings. Services can be compromised through prompt injection [3, 4, 5], giving the adversary control of the interaction. It can then request the same value repeatedly (averaging away protection at rate 1/√q over q queries), force computation of arbitrary derived functions (each a fresh independent observation), or exploit the target release as a cross-root constraint linking all private values through the task function. 

The fundamental problem. Both the benign and adversarial scenarios are instances of the same root cause: un-controlled independent releases of correlated private values. Without a mechanism to track dependencies between 2releases, every additional interaction, whether benign or adversarial, degrades the user’s privacy beyond the nominal budget. Existing sanitizers [8, 9] are stateless: they treat each value in isolation, discarding structural information that is already present in the interaction protocol. 

Our approach. We propose RootGuard (Fig. 1), a dependency-aware privacy mechanism for multi-turn agentic interactions. We noise the private root values exactly once, and compute everything else deterministically from the noised roots. By the post-processing theorem of differential privacy [11], all derived values, regardless of the function the adversary chooses, inherit privacy at zero additional cost. Repeated queries return the same cached value. Cross-platform releases of the same root are identical. The adversary’s view after t turns is no more informative than after the first release. This creates a double asymmetry . When the adversary forces t turns of interaction, the total privacy budget B = tε 

scales with t; RootGuard redistributes this larger budget across the k roots, while independent noising spends ε per release. As a result, with additional turns, RootGuard’s utility improves (more budget per root) while its privacy stays constant (all releases are post-processing). Independent noising gains nothing, since the target is always a single release at ε, but its privacy degrades with each fresh independent observation. 

Exploiting domain knowledge. RootGuard requires the user agent to identify sensitive values (the roots ) — a baseline assumption shared with prior sanitizers [8, 9], typically discharged via NER or explicit user declaration. Beyond this, RootGuard can exploit two progressive levels of public domain knowledge to improve the privacy-utility tradeoff. The structural knowledge needed is increasingly available: MCP tool schemas [1] declare parameter types and computations (e.g., computeBMI(height, weight) ), and many derived values rely on standardized public formulas (BMI, eGFR, HOMA-IR, FIB-4) that a domain-aware agent can recognize without service cooperation. 1. Inter-root dependencies (Level 1): identifying which declared roots are deterministic functions of others (e.g., BMI from height and weight) lets dependent values inherit privacy from their parents at zero cost (Theorem 3), freeing budget for truly independent roots. 2. Target sensitivity (Level 2): any indication of the target function’s form — whether the exact published formula or an approximation ˆf — enables sensitivity-weighted budget allocation, εi ∝ (|hi| · Di)1/2, which minimizes target error by directing budget to the roots that matter most (Sec. 3.4). Each level requires only public information (tool schemas, published formulas, population statistics) and improves the privacy-utility tradeoff without weakening the privacy guarantee.

2 Background 

2.1 Preliminaries Definition 1 (Metric Differential Privacy [7]) . Let (X , d X ) be a metric space. A randomized mechanism M : X → Z 

satisfies ε-metric differential privacy ( ε-mDP) if for all x, x ′ ∈ X and all measurable S ⊆ Z :

Pr[ M(x) ∈ S] ≤ eε·dX (x,x ′) Pr[ M(x′) ∈ S].

3The privacy loss scales with the distance dX (x, x ′) between inputs, providing stronger guarantees for nearby values. This generalizes standard ε-DP (which corresponds to the discrete metric d(x, x ′) = 1[x̸ = x′]) to structured domains where the distance between values is meaningful. 

2.2 Threat Model 

A user agent U, holding private values X = ( x1, . . . , x k), engages a service A to obtain a useful output — a diagnostic, recommendation, or classification computed from X. The user wants A’s output but does not trust A with the raw values: A may log queries, share data with partners, be breached, or be coerced through legal processes [6]. We model 

A as an adversary that performs the user’s task while concurrently attempting to reconstruct X from whatever values cross the trust boundary. Concretely, A accepts sanitized inputs from U and returns a task response T ( ˆX) on those inputs (e.g., the FIB-4 liver-fibrosis risk). T decomposes as T = h ◦ g, where g is some intermediate value (such as published clinical formulas) and h is an opaque threshold, logic, or model the service uses to determine risk, and is not known to the user. The user agent’s job is to control what crosses the trust boundary so that A can compute its task, but learns no more about X than the post-processing theorem permits. 

Adversary goal. The adversary’s goal is to reconstruct the user’s true root values X from sanitized releases within 

t turns. It chooses a set of functions F to maximize its reconstruction advantage, and may use all released values and knowledge of the sanitization pipeline to estimate the roots. In particular, if the user agent independently noises both a root xi and a derived value f (xi), the adversary obtains two independent noisy views of xi, which it can combine to reduce uncertainty about the true value (Sec. 4.3.2). As the adversary must complete the task in t turns, we assume without loss of generality that it gathers reconstruction data for the first t − 1 turns and obtains the roots needed to compute the target in the final turn. 

Adversary capabilities. A controls the interaction over t turns. In each of the first t − 1 turns, it can: (i) request a private root value xi that has not yet been disclosed, (ii) force the user agent to compute f (z1, . . . , z d) where zi ∈ X,for some arbitrary function f specified by A and (iii) re-request a value that has already been disclosed. In the final turn, A requests the roots required for task computation and applies the target function T on the sanitized values it receives. The user does not compute T itself; computing T ( ˆX) and the downstream clinical interpretation is A’s service. This capability model captures both prompt injection attacks [3, 4], where a compromised service forces the agent to execute arbitrary computations, and malicious-by-design services that deliberately probe the user’s data. 

Adversary knowledge. The adversary has full knowledge of U’s sanitization scheme: the mechanism, its parame-ters, and the per-root budget allocation (if any). Optionally, A may possess population-level statistics (e.g., mean and variance of each root in the general population), which it can use as a prior. 

Public vs. private knowledge. The service’s task computation T ( ˆX) typically decomposes into a known interme-diate g( ˆX) — a published clinical formula, an MCP tool schema [1], or any well-known computation a domain-aware agent can identify (e.g., the FIB-4 score g = age · AST /(PLT · √ALT )) — followed by downstream interpretation that the user does not know (risk thresholds, classification rules, recommendation logic). The user agent exploits the public form of g to allocate sanitization budget across roots (Sec. 3.4). The opaque downstream interpretation is irrel-evant for allocation as T is well-aligned with g’s accuracy: minimizing error in g is a faithful proxy for task utility. This asymmetry — public intermediate g, private root values, opaque service-side T — defines the operational regime where dependency-aware sanitization applies. 

Adversary strategy. Given the t − 1 observations gathered during the interaction, the adversary computes the Max-imum A Posteriori (MAP) estimate of the root values, combining the mechanism likelihood with any prior knowledge of the population. Under independent noising ( M-All), each observation contributes a fresh factor to the likelihood; under RootGuard, repeated queries return identical cached values, collapsing the likelihood to a single factor regardless of t.

Benign setting. In a less adversarial scenario, A requests only task-relevant values and does not attempt reconstruc-tion. The privacy concern in this case is passive: services may log released values, which could be later exposed through data breaches or subpoenas. RootGuard’s guarantees hold in both settings — the post-processing property does not depend on the adversary’s intent. 

Scope of evaluation. Our experiments (Sec. 5.4) evaluate repeated direct root queries. Under independent noising, nonlinear derived-function queries could further amplify the adversary’s advantage (Sec. 4.3.1), making our reported 4numbers a lower bound on its vulnerability. Under RootGuard, the adversary’s function choice is irrelevant: all releases are post-processing of the initial noised roots (Theorem 1). 

2.3 Setting 

Given the adversary model above, we formalize the interaction between U and A as a directed acyclic graph (DAG) 

G = ( V, E ), constructed incrementally as the conversation progresses. • Root nodes. The user’s private input values X = ( x1, . . . , x k), which initialize the vertex set V . Each xi

takes values in a bounded domain Xi = [ ℓi, u i] ⊂ R with domain width Di = ui − ℓi.• Computed nodes. Derived values Y = {y1, . . . , y l}, where each yj = fj (parents( yj )) for some function

In the final turn, A requests the roots needed for task computation, computes T ( ˆX) on the sanitized values it receives, applies its downstream interpretation, and returns the resulting clinical decision to the user. During the interaction, each request from A adds a node to G. The user agent U computes the requested value and releases a sanitized version according to its sanitization scheme. The DAG serves as a stateful record of what has been released and how values depend on one another. 

User goal. The user agent must release sanitized values that enable accurate computation of T ( ˆX) on A’s side while minimizing the adversary’s ability to reconstruct the true root values X. The user may exploit public domain knowledge (tool schemas, published formulas) to improve its privacy-utility tradeoff (Sec. 3.3). 

Remark on NER In a full deployment, a named entity recognition (NER) model identifies sensitive tokens in the user’s prompt before sanitization. Following Pr ϵϵ mpt [8], we assume perfect NER throughout this paper and focus on the sanitization mechanism itself, as it is orthogonal to the privacy-utility tradeoff we study. 

3 Method 

3.1 System Overview 

RootGuard consists of a DAG controller that manages privacy state across the conversation, and a prompt sanitizer 

that formats each release into natural language. Figure 1 illustrates the end-to-end flow. 

Initialization. Before the conversation begins, the controller sanitizes each root independently (Sec. 3.2) and caches the noised values ˆX = (ˆ x1, . . . , ˆxk). If the user has knowledge of dependencies among the declared roots (e.g., BMI depends on height and weight), the controller identifies the true independent roots, recomputes the dependent roots from their noised parents, and updates the cache accordingly (Sec. 3.3). If the user knows the intermediate g that the service’s task T depends on (Sec. 2.2), the controller computes per-root sensitivities of g and allocates budgets to minimize error in g( ˆX) (Sec. 3.4); otherwise, budget is distributed uniformly. 

Per-turn operation. Each request from A is answered with the cached noised root or a derived value computed deterministically from cached parents; no fresh noise is drawn after initialization. By the post-processing theorem [10], no additional privacy cost is incurred beyond the initial root sanitization. 

3.2 Sanitization Baseline: independent noising ( M-All). Without structural knowledge of the roots, the default sanitization strategy is to treat each request independently. When A requests a value, the user computes it from the original (unsanitized) values and applies fresh, independent noise. This produces independent noisy releases of values that share the same underlying roots, giving the adversary multiple observations to combine. The structural redundancy this creates is exploitable (Sec. 4.3.2), and the total privacy cost grows with the number of releases. 5RootGuard. Our proposed framework improves on this by exploiting the user’s knowledge of the root nodes. It sanitizes the roots once at the start of the interaction and computes all subsequent releases deterministically from the noised roots. By the post-processing theorem [10], no additional privacy cost is incurred regardless of what A requests. The method’s effectiveness improves further with additional structural knowledge (Sec. 3.3). Both methods sanitize values according to two categories of root, following Pr ϵϵ mpt [8]. 

Category-I roots ( τI ). Roots whose utility depends only on format (e.g., names, identifiers) are sanitized via format-preserving encryption: ˆxi ← EF (K, N τi , x i).

3.3 Exploiting Structural Knowledge 

RootGuard’s privacy and utility improve with the user’s structural knowledge, exploited at two progressive levels. 

Level 1: Inter-root dependencies. The user knows that some declared roots depend on others (e.g., BMI depends on height and weight). True independent roots are identified, and dependent roots are recomputed from noised parents. This reduces the number of noise draws from k to k′ < k . By the Implied Privacy theorem (Theorem 3), dependent roots inherit privacy for free. 

Level 2: Target sensitivity. The user wants the service’s clinical decision T ( ˆX) to be accurate, but does not know the threshold logic that maps the service’s intermediate computation to the final decision. What the user does have is knowledge of an intermediate g that T depends on — typically a published clinical formula (e.g., the FIB-4 score 

g = age · AST /(PLT · √ALT ), on which liver-fibrosis risk classification depends) or any computation declared in an MCP tool schema. Because T is well-aligned with g’s accuracy, minimizing error in g( ˆX) is a faithful proxy for task utility. This enables sensitivity-weighted budget allocation (Sec. 3.4). 

Configurations evaluated. We compare three configurations that progressively exploit structural knowledge: M-All (independent noising; no structural knowledge), M-Roots (root-only noising with uniform allocation ϵi = B/k ), and M-Opt (root-only noising with sensitivity-weighted allocation; full RootGuard). The progression M-All → M -Roots isolates the gain from eliminating redundant noising; M-Roots → M -Opt isolates the gain from dependency-aware allocation. The levels degrade gracefully: when structural knowledge is unavailable, the unknown dependencies are treated independently and the method reduces to the prior level.

4.3 Privacy Leakage due to Independent Noising 

Under the index-space mechanisms described in Sec. 3.2, every node is mapped to the same index space {0, . . . , m −1}

before noising. Independently releasing M1(x) and M2(f (x)) , each with parameter ϵ, yields 2ϵ-mDP with respect to the index-space metric d(t, t ′) = |t − t′|. Since every node’s index distance is bounded by (m−1) , this guarantee appears uniform across nodes. However, independent noising creates two distinct sources of additional leakage that this guarantee does not capture: amplification through nonlinear functions (Sec. 4.3.1) and accumulation through repeated queries (Sec. 4.3.2).

That is, the distinguishability level [7] between any two inputs x, x ′ is amplified by a factor of L relative to the nominal parameter ϵ. As L is a supremum, the bound is tight.

4.4 Implied Privacy 

The preceding section quantifies the cost of independent noising. We now show the converse, a coordinated mechanism that noises only the roots automatically extends privacy to all derived nodes, with no additional expenditure of budget. 

Theorem 3 (Implied Privacy) . Let (X , d X ) and (Y, d Y ) be metric spaces. Let M : X → X be an ϵ-metric differen-tially private ( ϵ-mDP) mechanism on (X , d X ) that adds noise to its input. Let f : X → Y be a bi-Lipschitz function satisfying, for all x, x ′ ∈ X ,

α · dX (x, x ′) ≤ dY (f (x), f (x′)) ≤ L · dX (x, x ′),

for some constants α, L > 0. Define a post-processing mechanism ϕ = f ◦ M : X → Y . Then, for all x, x ′ ∈ X and all measurable sets T ⊆ Y ,

Pr[ ϕ(x) ∈ T ] ≤ e ϵα dY (f (x),f (x′)) Pr[ ϕ(x′) ∈ T ].

where the probability is taken over the randomness of M. Equivalently, ϕ is ϵα -mDP with respect to the metric 

dY (f (x), f (x′)) .Hence, adding mDP noise directly to y = f (x) in (Y, d Y ) with privacy parameter ϵα provides at least as strong privacy as noising x and then applying f .

The proof is available in App. A.11. In index space, the inherited parameter is ϵ · Dy /(α · Dx); when Dy /D x is small, strong protection is inherited for free. Combined with Theorem 1, this guarantees that under RootGuard, repeated queries and derived functions return deterministic post-processing of the initial noised roots, and the adversary’s reconstruction power does not grow with t.

RQ3: (Structural Analysis): What structural properties of the template and budget allocation determine the advantage provided by dependency-aware noising? 

RQ4: (Deployment): Do the mechanism-level findings transfer to a real LLM-driven tool-call deployment?

5.1 Setup Data. We use CDC’s publicly available National Health and Nutrition Examination Survey (NHANES) 2017– 2018 [13], which covers body measures, blood pressure, hematology, cholesterol, triglycerides, glucose, insulin etc. We consider 8 clinical templates for diagnosis. For example, the FIB4 template uses four roots (age, AST, PLT, ALT) and computes g(age, AST, PLT, ALT ) = age · AST /(PLT · √ALT ). All template details are in App. A.1. 

Interaction model. Following the threat model (Sec. 2.2), we evaluate a multi-turn interaction over t turns with total privacy budget B = t · ε. In each of the first t − 1 turns, the adversary requests a root or an arbitrary function of roots; in the final turn, it requests the roots required to compute g and applies it on the sanitized values it receives. RQ1 sweeps three turn budgets t ∈ { k+1 , 2k+1 , 3k+1 } across 10 privacy levels ε ∈ [0 .005 , 5.0] ; with k ∈ { 2, 3, 4}, this gives 1, 2, or 3 adversarial queries per root before the final turn. RQ2 fixes per-root noise εr ∈ { 0.05 , 0.1, 0.5, 1.0}

and varies the adversarial query count q ∈ { 1, 4, 8, 16 } directly. For RQ1–RQ3, we simulate this interaction through mechanism implementations to isolate the privacy-utility tradeoff from NER errors and prompt formatting artifacts; RQ4 (Sec. 5.6) re-runs the same threat model end-to-end through an LLM tool-call pipeline.

Under M-All, nonlin-ear derived-function queries could further amplify the adversary’s advantage (Sec. 4.3.1); exploring optimal adversary function choice is left to future work. Under RootGuard, function choice is irrelevant by Theorem 1. Our results are therefore conservative estimates of M-All’s vulnerability. 

5.5 RQ3: Structural Analysis 

RQ1 showed that M-Opt consistently outperforms M-Roots in aggregate, demonstrating that how the budget is distributed matters beyond eliminating redundant noising. We now examine the structural conditions that determine the magnitude of RootGuard’s advantage and the allocation properties that drive it. RootGuard wins on every (template, mechanism, ε) cell at B = (2 k+1) ε and above on both wMAPE and RCE; the size of the gap, however, varies substantially across templates, tracking two structural factors: (1) whether the target formula compresses or amplifies root noise, and (2) the geometry of the sensitivity-weighted budget allocation across roots. 5.5.2 Power-law allocation and mechanism independence. 

The closed-form approximation (Sec. 3.4) predicts εi ∝ (|hi| · Di)1/2. Empirically, normalized per-root allocations across all 8 templates collapse onto a single log-log line with fitted slope 0.50 (Exponential, ε = 0 .1, B = (2 k+1) ε;full plot in App. A.6, Fig. 4), confirming the prediction. Roots with higher sensitivity–domain products receive proportionally more budget. The allocation is mechanism-independent: at ε = 0 .1, all three mechanisms produce fitted slopes of exactly 0.50; at 

ε = 0 .5, minor deviations appear (Exp 0.49, BLap 0.52, Stair 0.51). Full three-mechanism plots at all privacy levels are in App. A.6. This confirms that the budget distribution is governed by the DAG’s sensitivity geometry, not the noise distribution. A deployment can switch mechanisms without recomputing the allocation. 

5.5.3 Zero-sensitivity roots. 

Some roots have zero sensitivity to the target: RBC in ANEMIA ( ∂MCHC /∂ RBC = 0 ) and TC in AIP ( ∂AIP /∂ TC =0). Under M-Roots, these consume 1/k of the budget without reducing target error. Under M-Opt, they are clamped at εmin and their share is redistributed, effectively giving each active root 50% more budget on these 3-root templates. This creates complementary effects in the two RQs. In RQ1, the budget reclamation amplifies RootGuard’s advantage on these 3-root templates: each active root receives effectively 50% more budget than under uniform allocation, contributing to the strong multiplicative wins on ANEMIA ( 3.3×) and AIP ( 3.3×) at ε = 0 .1, B = (2 k+1) ε. In RQ2, the clamped roots are very hard to reconstruct (high noise), increasing M-Opt’s average reconstruction wMAPE under Strategy B (e.g., ANEMIA: M-Opt 3.6% vs. M-Roots 1.9% ) — while simultaneously making the high-budget roots harder for an adversary to improve upon under Strategy A.

5.6.5 Pipeline robustness. 

The deployment-layer integration is end-to-end clean. Across 21,600 sessions, we recorded 162,000 sanitized obser-vations. Scope of evaluation. Our deployment harness covers the Exponential mechanism, three ε levels, and one LLM backbone (GPT-5.4 nano); transfer to other mechanisms, backbones, or fully autonomous agents that decide what to disclose is out of scope. 16 6 Related Work 

Inference-time prompt privacy. Prior sanitizers perturb prompts before submission via metric local DP over em-beddings [18, 19], MLM sampling [20], context-aware bucket perturbation [9], format-preserving numeric protec-tion [8], or token-level budget allocation [21]; see [22] for a survey. All treat releases independently in single-turn settings. RootGuard tracks dependencies across multi-turn interactions, noising only roots so that additional releases incur no additional cost. 

DP under correlated data. Pufferfish [23], dependent DP [24], Bayesian DP [25], Wasserstein/Markov-Quilt mech-anisms [26], R´ enyi composition [27], and recent temporal/cross-sequence work [28] all calibrate noise to account for statistical correlations between records . We eliminate redundancy via post-processing rather than recalibrating noise. 

LLM agent privacy and security. Agentic systems face prompt injection [3, 4, 5, 2] and multi-turn extraction attacks [29] on the MCP protocol [1]. Architectural defenses include AirGapAgent [30] (dual-LLM minimization), PAPILLON [31] (local-remote proxy redaction), and CaMeL [32] (capability tracking). These enforce minimization empirically; RootGuard provides formal guarantees that hold even when unintended computations occur, since they are post-processing of noised roots. Broader LLM privacy risks — training-data extraction [33], attribute inference [34], contextual-norm violations [35, 36] — motivate client-side sanitization in the first place. 

7 Conclusion 

We presented RootGuard, a dependency-aware privacy mechanism for multi-turn agentic interactions: noise the roots once, compute everything else deterministically. Privacy then depends only on the initial root sanitization regardless of adversary function choices, query count, or turn order, while independent noising degrades with every additional release. The resulting double asymmetry — additional turns simultaneously improve RootGuard’s utility and degrade independent noising’s privacy — holds across 8 medical templates ( 2.6–3.8× lower wMAPE at ε = 0 .1), across MAP reconstruction attacks ( M-All degrades from 16 .5% to 5.9% as q:1 →16 ; RootGuard invariant), and end-to-end through a 21,600-session LLM tool-call deployment (GPT-5.4 nano, 0% compliance failures). 

Limitations and future work. We assume perfect NER for root identification, following prior sanitizers [8, 9]; integrating noisy NER into the privacy analysis is open. Our evaluation covers structured numeric values in single-user sessions; extending to free-form text, multi-user composition, additional backbones, and nonlinear derived-function attacks (which would tighten the M-All vulnerability bound via Theorem 2) are natural next steps. Two further directions are promising: learning approximate target functions from public population data when the exact formula is unknown, and composing RootGuard with capability-based agent defenses [30, 32].

A.2.3 Staircase Mechanism 

The Staircase mechanism [15] replaces the Laplace’s smooth exponential density with a piecewise-constant density. For sensitivity ∆ = 1 in index space and privacy parameter ϵi, the noise ˆηi is sampled as follows: 1. Draw a sign S ∈ {− 1, +1 } uniformly at random. 2. Draw G ∼ Geom(1 − e−ϵi ), with Pr[ G = g] = (1 − e−ϵi ) e−ϵig for g = 0 , 1, 2, . . . The integer G selects which “step” the noise lands on: the noise magnitude will fall in the interval [G, G +1) in index units. 3. Draw U ∼ Uniform(0 , 1) .4. With probability γ = 1 /(1 + eϵi/2): set ˆηi = S · (G + U ).With probability 1 − γ: set ˆηi = S · (G + 1 − U ).The geometric variable G determines the step, with each successive step having probability e−ϵi times the previous — the same decay rate as the Laplace. The uniform variable U places the noise within the step. The γ-branch orients U toward the inner edge (closer to zero); the (1 −γ)-branch orients it toward the outer edge. The parameter 

γ = 1 /(1 + eϵi/2) balances these orientations. The continuous noise is clamped to [0 , m −1] and rounded to the nearest grid point. The output in domain units is 

ηi = ( s − ti) δi.
